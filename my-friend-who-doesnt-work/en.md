# My Friend Who Doesn't Work

"I did some rehab work today, for the first time in a while," he says at the bar. He's a photographer.

He lifts his thick glass mug slightly. The foam on the amber beer catches the lamplight and glows white. The inside of the bar looks like something out of an American Western. Swinging saloon doors, wooden pillars with visible knots, a long counter, antlers mounted on the wall. In the back, a weathered jukebox plays softly, and behind the counter a mustachioed bartender silently lines up polished glasses. The floorboards creak dryly each time someone walks.

Every time the word "work" comes out of his mouth, I still feel a small jolt of surprise. And the fact that he prefixes it with "rehab" makes it all the more striking.

"What did you shoot?"

"Family portraits for Shichi-Go-San. Three hours."

"Sounds like you were actually working."

"No, it was bad. Three hours and I was wiped out. I felt like a wanted man spotted by the sheriff."

"You're letting the décor get to your head."

When I said that, he laughed and took a drink of his beer. A bit of foam clung to the rim of his mug. He'd always had well-defined features, but now they carried a trace of life's weariness. Still, the way he looked you straight in the eye despite being tired—that hadn't changed.

I met him in college. I was in the engineering department; he was in the arts. Our classes and buildings barely overlapped, yet somehow we always ended up on the same bench. I'd go there to drink canned coffee between classes; he'd sit there without his camera, just watching people. He used to say, "Everyone looks like they're in a hurry."

After graduation, I got a regular job. Wake up, ride the train, sit through meetings, answer emails. He kept taking photographs. For the first few years he scraped by with wedding shoots and small magazine gigs, but eventually he stopped talking much about work. His social media filled up with photos of playground equipment in empty parks, fogged-up windows, the undersides of elevated highways at dusk.

"Have you been shooting lately?"

"Yeah. Not the paid kind, though."

"Think any of it could sell?"

"The photos that look like they'd sell are usually boring."

"That sounds like sour grapes."

"Half of it is."

He was honest. He probably didn't even have the energy to put up a front.

He's thirty-two, with no steady job. Part-time work never sticks. A few times a month, he picks up a shoot through connections. What that doesn't cover, he manages with occasional sales of old photographs and a gentle trickle of support from his parents. By society's standards, he'd probably be classified squarely as "someone who doesn't work."

And yet I've never been able to think of him as entirely lazy.

He's bad with mornings, bad with deadlines, and even worse with phone calls. But when it comes to seeing light, his eyes turn deadly serious. He's the kind of guy who shows up late to meet you and explains that the shadows on the side of the road were beautiful. Most people would take that as nothing more than an excuse, but for him it was probably the truth.

On top of that, he has a habit of suddenly getting an idea and throwing himself into it with full force in some unexpected direction. It must have been around the end of his twenties—he announced, "I want to properly master Chinese in Taiwan," and abruptly left for a three-month language program in Taipei. I assumed he was going to study photography, but he actually attended a language school every day, drilling pronunciation from scratch. After he came back, he could order at restaurants and ask for directions in fairly natural Chinese. But I never heard that he'd turned that language ability into any kind of stable work. That was just like him.

"Who told you to do rehab?"

"My mother."

"That's harsh."

"No, she's kind. It hits harder because she's kind."

He traced a finger along the condensation on the side of his mug.

"She said, 'If you keep going like this, you'll end up hating even the things you love.'"

I was quiet for a moment. The bartender set down a glass of water without a word. He murmured a small thanks and took a sip.

"So how was it? Working again after so long."

"The kid wouldn't listen at all."

"Of course not."

"But at the very end, there was one good shot. The parents had half given up, and the kid was tired too, and there was this moment where he suddenly went completely still, just a straight face."

"A straight face was the good one?"

"Yeah. It was the kind of face where you could tell—ah, this kid is really loved in this family."

That's the kind of thing he says. He talks as though he can see something in a person's face that even they don't know about yet.

Back in college, I once looked at his photographs and felt a flash of irritation. It wasn't about skill. It was the feeling that he was actually seeing the world I only thought I was seeing. I probably still hold a small grudge about that.

"You know," I said, "if you can see that much, you should really get your act together."

After I said it, I wondered if it was too harsh. But he didn't get angry.

"Get my act together, huh."

"As a career."

"If I could do that, I wouldn't be struggling."

"Everyone struggles and does it anyway."

"Yeah."

He nodded simply.

"That's really true."

When he said it like that, I was the one who ran out of words. If he'd pushed back, I could have ridden the momentum of being right. But he didn't give me that.

There was a long silence. The swinging doors opened, and a stranger walked in with a gust of wind. Boot heels clacked against the floorboards, then it was quiet again. Like a scene from a movie, I thought.

He suddenly laughed and said:

"But you know, even people who don't work have their own kind of busy."

"What do you mean?"

"Like forgetting to hang the laundry and hating yourself for it. Or misjudging the markdown timing at the afternoon supermarket. Or how everything about your life suddenly feels hopeless when evening hits."

"That last one isn't being busy."

"It is. Busy in here." He tapped his chest.

I laughed too. It was silly. But I sort of understood.

This past year, I'd been doing reasonably well at work. I had junior staff under me now, and things were running smoothly. But on Sunday nights, I'd get the feeling that I'd left something important behind and kept moving forward without it. I couldn't put into words what I'd left behind. It was the kind of unease that disappears by Monday morning.

"Doesn't it scare you? Not working?"

He thought for a moment before answering.

"It scares me. A lot."

"Then why?"

"Probably because there's something that scares me more."

"What?"

"Going through the motions of working and losing the feeling of being alive."

I set my mug down. It made a dull sound on the wooden counter.

I thought that was an unfair thing to say. As if everyone who works has lost their sense of being alive. That's not true. Work has saved me before. There were times when having the shape of busyness around me was the only thing keeping me on my feet.

But at the same time, I knew his words weren't entirely untrue.

"Well, in my case, I'm probably just dressing up clumsiness as philosophy."

He shrugged.

"There's a good chance of that."

"A high chance."

"Pretty high."

"Stop it."

We both laughed again.

The bar's wall clock had passed eleven. The bartender was quietly polishing glasses. He pulled a digital camera from his bag and turned the screen toward me.

"Want to see the one from today?"

It was a small boy in a kimono. The shrine in the background was slightly out of focus, and the boy wasn't looking straight at the camera. His gaze drifted slightly to the side. His lips were pressed together. And yet I couldn't look away. It was a strong photograph. Not because it was cute, but because you could feel that a real, whole person was standing right there.

"That's good."

I said it honestly.

"Right?"

"If you can take shots like this, you can work."

"Those are two different things."

He said it without sounding particularly sorry.

I paid the bill. As always. He said, "I'll pay you back when I get a good photo gig," and I replied, "I've lost count of how many times I've heard that line."

We pushed through the swinging doors and stepped outside. The night air was a little cold. An old lantern-style light was mounted on the exterior wall, casting a pale circle on the sidewalk. Even though it was the middle of the city, time seemed to move a little slower in front of that bar.

Waiting for the light at the intersection, he said:

"You know what I thought about today, while working?"

"What?"

"Being needed by someone once in a while—it really does feel good."

The way he said it stayed with me in a strange way. He wasn't boasting about work or rejecting it. He was just isolating the texture of being needed and holding it up to the light.

The signal turned green and we started walking.

Just before we parted at the station, he readjusted his camera bag on his shoulder and said:

"But hey, you're working hard too, you know."

"Where'd that come from?"

"I was watching you earlier and I thought—listening to a guy like me without running away, that takes real stamina."

"That's the first time anyone's given me that kind of review."

"Work isn't just what you do at the office. You're good at listening to people. Probably always have been."

I found myself making a vague face, neither smiling nor denying it.

"Don't make things up."

"I'm not making it up. Photography—it only really becomes a photograph when someone sees it. That shot from today, when you said 'that's good,' it made me feel like I'd actually done some real work today."

After saying that, he looked away, a little embarrassed.

"So, yeah. I'm the one who's been helped here."

That one line sank deeper than I expected.

Lately I'd been feeling like I was replaceable no matter what I did. I was supposedly valued at work, but the documents, the meetings, the emails—if someone else did them tomorrow, the world probably wouldn't notice. I hadn't been able to find the right name for that feeling.

But his words were strangely specific. Not some grand encouragement, but the kind of thing that tells you, right here and now, that you mattered to someone.

"I'll report back next time I do rehab," he said.

"Make it regular."

"I'll do my best."

"That's the least trustworthy answer there is."

"Well then, you too."

"Me what?"

"You should be a little better at giving yourself credit."

I didn't know what to say, so I just said, "That's hard."

"Just do rehab."

"For what?"

"For being alive."

He laughed. This time, I laughed honestly too.

He raised a hand and disappeared down the dark road leading away from the station. Probably heading home. Or maybe he'd stop along the way, having spotted some light worth capturing.

I passed through the ticket gates and climbed to the platform. While waiting for the train, I kept turning his words over in my mind. You're good at listening to people. I'm the one who's been helped. You should give yourself more credit.

On any other day, I probably wouldn't have been able to accept any of those head-on. But tonight, they settled into my chest with a strange ease. Being told those things by a friend who doesn't work, in a weird bar that looks like a Western saloon—it was a little absurd. But maybe that's exactly why it didn't feel like a lie.

The train slid into the platform. The doors opened. I stepped in and caught my reflection in the window. Tired, yes. But my face looked a little better than it had before I'd come out tonight.

Just before departure, my phone buzzed. A message from him.

"Thanks for today. Also, I looked at that photo again and it really is good."

I thought for a moment, then replied.

"Glad to hear it. Go work properly next time too."

The message was read immediately, and he wrote back:

"Let's both take it easy, one step at a time."

The train pulled out into the night city. Outside the window, the lights of people still working flowed past, alongside the darkness of shops already closed and the glow of streetlamps belonging to neither.

Watching those lights, I thought I'd probably go to the office the same way tomorrow. But it didn't feel like before—just being carried along by the current. Even without finding the right words, receiving someone's story, looking at someone's photograph and saying "that's good"—maybe those things have meaning too.

It wasn't a great leap forward. But I felt like I could arrive at my station sitting just a little straighter.

And for tonight, that was enough.

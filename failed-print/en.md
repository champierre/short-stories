# The Day He Took Home the Failed Print

Until he opened the door to the Aogaku Tsukumana Lab, Minato had thought of 3D printers as nothing more than slightly expensive-looking machines you might find in a university lab.

Minato, a second-year middle school student, was half-reading a flyer handed out in the classroom after school.
“Try designing with Tinkercad and bringing your ideas to life with a 3D printer.”

It was not that he disliked making things. He actually liked crafts well enough, and he enjoyed building plastic models too. But those were fun because the final shape had already been decided. Coming up with a form on his own felt difficult somehow.

“Why don’t you go?”

Mio, peering at the flyer from the next seat, said it casually.

“You seem like the kind of person who’d be good at this, Minato. You like detailed work.”

“It’s not about being good at it. Stuff like this takes taste, doesn’t it?”

“People who say ‘it takes taste’ are usually just running away before they even start.”

She laughed when she said it, and that irritated him just enough.
On impulse, Minato submitted the application form.

The Aogaku Tsukumana Lab felt much freer than he had expected. There were laptops on the tables, spools of filament in different colors, small prototype objects, and half-assembled parts of who-knew-what. It was tidy, but it still felt a little like a secret base.

Near the shelves stood the 3D printers the lab used. Someone was saying that the lab had replaced the AnkerMake M5 and M5C that had once been its main workhorses with Bambu Lab printers, which were cheaper and yet performed remarkably well. Minato did not understand the finer differences yet, but he could tell that the people in the lab were excited about the change.

The first time he touched Tinkercad, it felt almost surprisingly simple. Place a cube or a cylinder, stretch it, cut it, combine it. That was all. And yet, it was fun to watch a shape slowly come into being on the screen.

“For your first project, it can be anything,”
said one of the university student lab staff.

“It can be practical, or it can be weird. The best thing to make is something you personally want.”

Looking around, Minato saw students making keychains, while others were designing strange creature-like ornaments. Mio had already started making a cat-shaped cable holder, adding ears and then deleting them, thickening the tail and then tilting her head at the result.

After some hesitation, Minato decided to make a small stand for holding shogi pieces upright. The tables they used for club activities were a bit cramped, and he thought it would be convenient to have a place to keep the pieces together while waiting for his turn.

He made a square base and added narrow grooves. It should have been a simple shape, yet it was harder than he had expected. Even if it looked convincing on the screen, a slight difference in width might mean the pieces would not actually fit.

“Maybe the grooves are too shallow,”
he muttered.

Mio glanced over from the next seat.

“Why don’t you print it and find out?”

“That’s exactly the problem. If it fails, it wastes time and material.”

“But if you don’t print it, you’ll never know whether it’s really wrong.”

For some reason, her words stayed with him.

He saved the data and sent it to the Bambu Lab A1 mini. A progress bar appeared on the small screen, and the nozzle began laying down filament in lines as thin as thread. The moment the first layer was drawn, Minato pushed back his chair and stood up without thinking.

“Whoa…”

The shape from the screen was actually appearing on the desk in front of him.
It felt far stranger than he had imagined.

A few dozen minutes later, the first piece was finished. When he picked up the stand, still faintly warm, it really was the same lines and surfaces he had been moving around with his mouse only a short while earlier, now solidified into something real.

But the instant he tried placing a shogi piece into it, Minato frowned.
The grooves were too shallow. A slight touch was enough to make the piece fall over.

“I knew it.”

Frustration spread bitterly through his mouth.
It had looked fine on the screen.
He had spent time on it.
And still, it had not worked.

At that moment, the university student lab staff member did not laugh at the failed print. If anything, he looked a little pleased.

“That’s good,” he said.

“No, it failed.”

“Which is exactly why it’s good. Now you know clearly what to fix next.”

Minato had no reply.

From that day on, the way after-school hours felt began to change.
Even at home, he found himself sketching shapes in the corners of his notebooks.
During class, he would look at an eraser and think, If the edges were rounded like this, it might be easier to hold.
At stationery stores, before he even looked at what was being sold, he started thinking, How would I make this myself?

For the second print, he deepened the grooves.
For the third, he thickened the base a little for stability.
For the fourth, he rounded the edges so it would feel better in the hand.

Every time he failed, it got just a little better than before.
That felt strangely fun, like leveling up in a game.

Before long, the shogi stand alone was no longer enough for him. A nameplate holder that could hang from a shelf in the clubroom. A small winding part to keep earphones from tangling. A pen rest that could attach to the corner of a desk. Every “It would be nice if this existed” that came into his head began lining up one after another in the Tinkercad workspace.

One day, the lab decided to hold a small exhibition.
Everyone would place their creations on the tables and explain them to visitors.

After some hesitation, Minato decided to bring his first failed print as well. It was a flimsy stand with grooves that were too shallow. Looking at it now, it seemed awkward and clumsy. But it somehow felt wrong to display only the finished version without it.

On the day of the exhibition, a child who looked about elementary-school age stopped in front of Minato’s table.

“These look the same, but are they different?”

“A little.”

Minato lined the two pieces up side by side.

“This one was the failed version. The grooves were too shallow, so the shogi pieces fell over easily. So with this one, I made them deeper and rounded the edges too.”

The child compared them carefully, then said,

“The failed one looks cool too. It feels like it’s still on the way.”

Still on the way.
For some reason, that made Minato happy.

A finished object was not the only thing that counted as a work.
The lines that had not gone right, the corners he had shaved away too much, the holes he had made the wrong size—everything was connected to the shape in front of him now.

As they were leaving, evening light streamed through the lab windows. The shadows of the prototypes stretched long across the tables, making each one look a little special.

“So, what are you making next?”
Mio asked while cleaning up.

Minato thought for a moment before answering.

“I haven’t decided yet. But there is something I want to make.”

“What?”

“Something people can use at school. Something genuinely useful. But I want to care about how it looks, too.”

Mio laughed.

“You’re completely hooked now.”

He was about to deny it, but stopped.
She was right.

He had started out just arranging squares and circles.
Yet somewhere along the way, he had become unable to step away from the feeling of seeing his own ideas take shape.
Something that had existed only in his head now had weight in his hand.
He had never known that could be this exciting.

Before leaving the lab, Minato paused for a moment beside the printer.
The Bambu Lab A1 mini had already received someone else’s data and had quietly begun laying down a new first layer.

From nothing, little by little, a form was being born.
Watching it, Minato felt as though something inside him was becoming new as well.

Since entering middle school, he had found himself thinking more and more in terms of whether he could do something or not. He had also started avoiding things that looked likely to end in failure. But here, failure was not the end. It became material for the next design.

Because it did not work, he thought.
Because he thought, he could change it a little the next time.
And because he changed it a little, a shape appeared that had not existed before.

It was a story about making things, and probably not only that.

Minato slung his backpack over his shoulder and put both the finished stand and the very first failed print into its case.
He decided to take them both home.

They were both things he had made.
One was the person he was now, and the other was the person he had been on the way there.

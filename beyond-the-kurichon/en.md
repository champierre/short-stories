# Beyond the Kurichon

Before she could carry it all the way through, her fingers gave way.

The arrow had not fully reached extension. Her aim had not settled into the gold. And yet the release came first. Watching the arrow jump forward and drop below the target, Mashiro stood there holding her breath.

“That was a kurichon, wasn’t it?”

Katagiri said it from the next target over.

Mashiro said nothing. She knew that without being told. She had not released after the clicker went. Her body had fled before it cut.

That was what everyone in the club called it: *kurichon*—short for *clicker chonbo*. Not an official term, but everyone understood it instantly. Releasing when it was not time yet. Ending the shot before it had arrived.

Mashiro lowered her bow and drew the next arrow from her quiver.

It was the second time that day.

The first had been even more obvious. She had drawn, felt herself rush before she even came into the aim, and the release had exploded out of her. This one was not quite that early. But since the clicker had not gone, it was the same thing. The difference was that the second one left a much worse feeling behind than the first.

I did it again.

The thought stayed cold behind her shoulders.

Mashiro had started using a clicker in the spring of her second year. At first it felt helpful. Her draw length became consistent. She hesitated less. The trigger for the release became clear. It felt as though she could shoot only when she had truly reached it.

But something changed near the end of summer.

She became afraid that it would not cut. Afraid of holding too long. She could not stay inside the aim long enough, could not wait for that last little bit. And so she began to release before it had gone.

At first it was only an occasional mistake.  
Then once it happened in one arrow, it started happening again in the next.  
And once that began, she started fearing the next shot before she even raised the bow.

Kurichon did not end with a single arrow.  
The shadow of one shot remained inside the next.

“Want to take a break?”

Katagiri asked.

“No.”
“You look stiff.”
“I know.”
“That’s exactly when it gets dangerous.”

Mashiro smiled a little, but the expression vanished at once.

Behind the shooting line, first-year students were coming back from arrow retrieval. The advisor’s voice drifted from far away, mixed with the sound of someone straightening the target mats. It was an ordinary afternoon practice. Nothing special. And yet inside Mashiro, each arrow felt more cornered than the last.

This time I’ll release only after it cuts, she thought.  
Then all she could think about was making it cut.  
Her aim became shallow.  
Her expansion stopped.  
The strain built up.  
And still the release came first.

The order was already broken before she even raised the bow.

Mashiro stepped off the line and walked over to the water tap. When she turned the faucet, the water was still cold with early spring. She ran it over her wrists, then pressed it to her forehead.

“What’s wrong?”

The voice behind her was Mikami’s. He was the outside coach who came only once a week.

“Nothing.”
“That’s not a nothing face.”
“I’m just getting kurichon.”

Mikami nodded. There was no surprise in it, and no attempt to comfort her.

“How many?”
“Two.”
“Since this morning?”
“Since this morning.”
“Then it’s still shallow.”

Mashiro looked up.

“Shallow?”
“When it gets deeper, you release before you even draw.”
“That’s not funny.”
“I’m not joking.”

Mikami looked not at her bow, but toward the shooting line.

“Kurichon is releasing before the clicker cuts. But the thing that starts first is not the release itself.”
“Then what is it?”
“The urge to end it.”

Mashiro said nothing.

“You start thinking it might not cut. That it might take too long. That you might miss. And when that happens, people choose ‘ending quickly’ over ‘ending correctly.’ Even if you don’t feel like you’re choosing it, your body goes that way.”
“...Yes.”
“So if you try to fix kurichon by suppressing the release alone, next you get stuck, or you collapse into holding.”

Mikami spoke quietly. He did not scold her. He only gave names to what was happening.

“So what am I supposed to do?”
“Don’t make ‘not releasing before the clicker’ your goal.”
“But if I don’t—”
“Your goal is to keep expanding to the end. Whether it cuts or not is the result.”

Mashiro felt irritation rise.

“I already know that.”
“You know it, but you’re scared, aren’t you?”
“...I am.”
“That’s fine. Don’t wait until you stop being scared before you draw.”

With that, Mikami went back toward the line.

It’s fine to be scared.

That was not what Mashiro had expected to hear. She had always thought fear had to disappear before she could shoot properly. She thought she had to keep a calm face. She thought she had to forget mistakes in order to move on.

But in reality, the more she tried to forget, the denser the failure became.  
The more she tried to erase it, the more her mind gathered around it.

Mashiro returned to the target line.

Nock the arrow.  
Stand.  
Raise.  
Draw.

She did not think, *Don’t release before the clicker.*  
She thought only about using her back all the way through.

Her aim wavered slightly. The sight moved beneath the gold. Her mind was not calm. She still thought it might happen again. Even so, she kept expanding.

It cut.

Then she released.

The arrow landed in the red, an eight. It was not good. But the order had held. That mattered more to her than the number.

The next arrow was a nine.  
The one after that she let down.  
And after that letdown, the fear grew again.

Maybe today would just keep repeating like this—better, then worse, then better again. She thought that. But part of her also thought that was all right. This was probably not the kind of thing that vanished at once. Kurichon was a mistake, but it was also a reaction that had soaked into the body. It was not shallow enough to disappear after one or two good arrows.

Then came the last end.

The sun was lower now, and the white of the target looked slightly dimmer. The wind was light. Katagiri had already finished and was watching from behind.

“Don’t try too hard to wrap this one up nicely,” she said.

“I know.”
“That face says you know.”
“What does that even mean?”
“It means you’re trying to wrap it up nicely.”

Mashiro laughed and reset her grip on the bow.

She wanted to finish cleanly.  
She did not want another kurichon today.  
She wanted one proper arrow to close with.

She already knew that wanting it too badly was dangerous. Strong wishes made the body search for shortcuts. It wanted to finish early rather than go all the way through.

So she stopped thinking about endings.

She accepted being inside a single shot, and nothing more.

Nock.  
Stand.  
Breathe.  
Raise.  
Draw.

Her right elbow felt a little low, so she corrected it. She lowered her left shoulder. She did not go out to meet the string with her face. She did not stop the expansion. Even when the sight wavered outside the gold, she did not force it inward.

It still had not cut.

Inside her chest, a thought rose: *Soon. Hurry.*  
Another followed: *Finish before this becomes unbearable.*  
And another: *If I just release, it will be easier.*

Mashiro felt all of it and still expanded one little step more.

It cut.

Then she released.

The arrow stood in the gold for a nine.

Not the center. Not the kind of arrow that made people gasp. And yet Mashiro could not move for a while after lowering her bow. More than the score, what mattered was that the order inside her had remained intact.

She had not ended it before the cut.  
She had not been overtaken by fear.  
She had stayed inside the shot to the end.

“Good.”

Katagiri said it softly behind her.

Mikami said nothing. He only nodded.

On the way back to the clubroom, the evening wind tugged at the sleeve of her jacket. Adjusting the strap of her bow case on her shoulder, Mashiro found herself thinking:

Maybe all this time she had mistaken the opposite of kurichon for a perfect shot. No shaking. No fear. A beautiful draw and an arrow in the middle.

But maybe that was not it.

What lay on the other side of kurichon was not the absence of failure.  
It was the ability to recover the order even after failing.

There would be days when she released before the cut.  
There might be another one tomorrow.  
Even so, she did not have to decide the shot was over before it began.  
Even if it broke apart halfway through, she could still build another arrow that went all the way.

Walking beside her, Katagiri asked,

“So what changed most today?”

Mashiro thought for a moment.

“I realized kurichon isn’t the end of everything.”
“So there’s something beyond it?”
“Yeah.”
“That sounds like a title.”
“Maybe it does.”

At the far edge of the field, the soccer team was still shouting. The sky was pale blue, and beyond the nets, spring clouds drifted slowly past.

As she walked, Mashiro thought again of that last arrow.

What lay beyond kurichon was not some dramatic conquest.  
It might come back tomorrow. In competition it might break her much more easily.

Even so, there was still something beyond it.

To take the self who had released before the cut,  
and lead that self one step farther.

Maybe continuing in the sport meant doing exactly that—  
picking up those small recoveries again and again,  
until one day they became your shooting.

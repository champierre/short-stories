11:11

Whenever I see the number 11, I think of clock problems from the days when I was studying for junior high school entrance exams.

It happens when the number 11 lights up on a platform sign at a train station. When an elevator stops on the eleventh floor. When I see a price tag at the supermarket that says 211 yen with tax. Every time 11 appears in the middle of ordinary life like that, somewhere in my mind the faint image of a round clock rises to the surface.

A circle drawn on white paper. Twelve careful marks around it. The minute hand and the hour hand.
And the teacher's voice.

"Between eight and nine o'clock, at what time do the minute hand and the hour hand overlap?"

I remember the classroom air always feeling a little dry. Maybe it was winter. The light outside the window was pale and bright, but the air was cold, and everyone gripped their pencils tightly. The desks at the cram school were a little narrower than the ones at school, so even the sound of the child next to you rubbing an eraser felt strangely close.

Some children were good at clock problems, and some were not.
I was not exactly bad at them, but I never liked them.

Tree-planting problems and travel-speed problems had scenery to them. Trees lined up somewhere. People were walking somewhere. But clock problems had nothing except a clock, literally. Hands moved expressionlessly across a circle. And yet, inside those expressionless movements, words like catch up, fall behind, and overlap somehow slipped in. That always felt strangely human to me.

The number 11, in particular, was tightly bound to clock problems.

The reason has to do with the difference in speed between the minute hand and the hour hand. The minute hand makes one full turn in 60 minutes. The hour hand makes one full turn in 12 hours, so in one hour it moves only one-twelfth of the clock face, which is the same as five minute marks. That means the minute hand moves one mark per minute, while the hour hand moves only one-twelfth of a mark per minute.
So the difference between them is that the minute hand moves 11⁄12 of a mark per minute faster than the hour hand.

In clock problems, you often think like this: how far apart are the two hands now, and how many minutes will it take for that gap to close at their relative speed? That is where 11 appears. For example, if the two hands are one mark apart, the time needed to close that gap is:

1 ÷ (11⁄12) = 12⁄11 minutes.

That is why 11 appears so often in the answers. To me, 11 was never just a number. It was that slightly impatient speed at which the minute hand chased the hour hand.

Of course, back when I was actually a test-prep student, I did not understand it so neatly. In reality, it was much blurrier than that. I just kept turning the pages of problem books, wondering, "Why 11?" and every time I looked at the explanation saying "the difference in speed is 11⁄12," the number 11 sank deeper into me.

Memory is strange. Sometimes numbers, sounds, or smells remain more vividly than the meaning itself.

For me, that thing was 11.

One evening, on my way home from work, I looked up at a clock in a shopping street and saw that it was 11:11. The four digits stood in a line and looked unnaturally well arranged. Everyone waiting at the light was looking down at their phones. I was the only one looking up at the clock.

Eleven. Eleven.
What a very clock-problem kind of time, I thought.

At that moment, the cram school I had not thought about in years came back to me all at once. The seat in the very back of the classroom. The sheet of successful admissions posted on the wall. The teacher's hand making circles with a red pen. The taste of lukewarm tea I secretly drank in the hallway during breaks. And a girl who always sat in the second row from the front.

She was good at clock problems.

As soon as a problem was written on the board, she would quickly sketch a small clock in the corner of her notebook and mark the positions of the hands. She was quick with answers too. And yet she never acted proud of it. When the teacher called on her, she would stand up with a faintly troubled look and explain the formula in a quiet voice.

"The minute hand catches up by 11⁄12 each minute."

For some reason, I liked the way she said that.
Maybe it was because she used the words catches up.

One day, there was one problem I simply could not solve, and even after class I kept staring at my notebook. While everyone else was packing their bags, she passed by my desk and stopped.

"You're still doing that?"

She was not laughing. She just sounded a little puzzled.

Trying to save face, I said, "No, I already get it."
She leaned over my notebook, then gave a small "oh."

"It's easier if you think about 11 first."

"11?"

"Yeah. The speed of the minute hand catching up to the hour hand. If you remember just that part, clock problems usually come undone."

That phrase—come undone—also felt very much like her.
Not solving a hard problem, but loosening a tangled thread.

In the margin of my notebook, she drew a round clock. It was surprisingly round. The circles I drew were always crooked somewhere, but hers looked as if she had used a compass.

"Now the hour hand is here, and the minute hand is here, right? So the gap is this much. And each minute, it shrinks by 11⁄12."

As she spoke, she drew a small line beneath the number 11⁄12.

"So you divide the gap by that."

"I forget why it's 11 every single time."

"But even if you forget, you'll remember again. It's a strange number."

A strange number.
She was right. Not 10, not 12, but 11. It seemed like it ought to divide things neatly, and yet it did not. Maybe that awkwardness was exactly what made it stay in memory.

"See you," she said, and went home.

That was all.
And yet, for some reason, only that scene never disappeared.

After I left cram school, went on to high school, graduated from university, and started working, I never used clock problems again. I was often pressed for time, but I never had to calculate the difference in speed between clock hands. I even stopped wearing a watch and lived by the numbers on my phone alone.

And still, 11 sometimes opens an old door.

November. Platform 11. Jersey number 11.
Each time, for just a second, I become my elementary school self again: the boy frowning at a difficult problem, unable to say honestly that he did not understand, remembering the side of the girl's face as she sketched a clock with quick certainty.

The strange thing is that I can no longer remember her name. Not her family name, not her given name. Both have vanished cleanly.
And yet her voice remains: "It's easier if you think about 11 first."

Sometimes words remain before names do.
Sometimes numbers remain before faces do.
That happens in life.

The clock in the shopping street had turned to 11:12.
11:11 had already passed in an instant.

The light changed to green, and the crowd started forward. I was about to walk too, but then I stopped. I was sure that next to the clock she had drawn in my notebook, she had added something in small pencil letters. Not a formula. Just some trivial little phrase. But no matter how hard I tried, I could not remember what it was.

I will probably never see her again.
Even if I did, maybe neither of us would recognize the other.
Still, if somewhere there is someone else who sees the number 11 and remembers clock problems the same way I do, that thought makes me a little glad.

Just as the minute hand chases the hour hand, my present self seems to be trying to catch up to an old memory.
But perhaps it is the other way around.
Perhaps one moment from long ago has been moving forward at a constant speed all this time, and only now has it finally caught up with me.

When I looked up, a white moon had appeared in a break between the clouds.
Round. A little like a clock.

As I walked, I murmured to myself in my head:
The minute hand moves 11⁄12 faster than the hour hand.

And then, suddenly, the phrase I could not remember rose to the surface.

There's no need to hurry. A clock always meets again.

Whether those words had really been written in her handwriting, or whether my own longing created them later, I no longer know.
But that sentence stayed with me far longer than any arithmetic explanation for an entrance exam.

Whenever I see the number 11, what I remember is not clock problems themselves.
It is the fact that the time until two things meet can be calculated.
That even when there is distance between them, the gap can steadily shrink.
That even in movements too small to see, the hands are still advancing toward a meeting.

Back then, I think I needed that.
And perhaps I still do now.

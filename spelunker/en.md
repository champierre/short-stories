# Spelunker

I found the word at a secondhand bookshop in front of the station. In the very back of the store, on the hundred-yen-each shelf, sat a stack of English-Japanese dictionaries with peeling spines. The pages had yellowed to brown, and the covers were warped as though they had once absorbed water. I was idly flipping through the S section when my hand stopped.

spelunker

Someone had circled the entry in pencil. Next to it, in bold, hasty handwriting, was this:

Spelunker!

I couldn't help but laugh.

For my generation, Spelunker was a video game name before it was ever an English word. An absurdly fragile hero. He died from the smallest ledge. He died from touching a bat. He died so easily it felt unfair, and yet somehow you kept trying. To this day, I'm not sure whether I liked or hated that game's protagonist as a kid. All I remember clearly is the darkness of descending underground, and the feeling that the only way to learn was to die.

As I was about to close the dictionary, a slip of paper fell from between the pages.

It was a scrap torn from a college-ruled notebook. On the blue lines, a few words had been jotted in pencil.

—July 23rd   Old air-raid shelter entrance   3 PM
—Flashlight, work gloves, rope
—Show N

Only the N was pressed a little darker.

I turned the slip over. Nothing. I put it back and looked again, but there was still nothing. Under the fluorescent lights of the bookshop, the handwriting looked strangely childish. Written with pure momentum, yet clearly important to whoever had written it.

I bought the dictionary on the spot.

---

On the train home, I kept unfolding and refolding the slip of paper. Old air-raid shelter entrance. Show N. The meaning was clear enough, but nothing clicked in my memory. And yet something stirred. I recognized this kind of stirring. It was like having a fragment of a melody stuck in your head without being able to recall the song's name.

Back home, I looked at the dictionary's annotation again.

Spelunker!

The handwriting seemed familiar. Of course, it might have been my imagination. There was no way to identify a child's handwriting from over thirty years ago. But deep in my chest, something was already settling into place.

---

The following Saturday, I went to the place described on the slip of paper.

At the edge of a residential area, a small thicket of trees remained, and behind it, the slope had been gouged out as if the earth had been scooped away. Parting the grass, I could just make out the edge of a concrete rim. I crouched down and peered in. A dark hole gaped open. It didn't look grand enough to call an old air-raid shelter. But it was a space a person could enter.

Despite the summer heat, cold air flowed from inside the hole.

I pulled a flashlight from my backpack. I put on work gloves. I hadn't brought rope, and I felt a small pang of regret about that. As a child, I would have made a huge fuss the moment I saw an entrance like this. Now, the crumbling footing and the dampness of the ceiling concerned me first.

I went in anyway.

A few steps in, my eyes began to adjust to the darkness. The walls were earth, with tree roots poking through here and there. A space that had been dug out and abandoned—neither clean nor dirty. Pebbles crunched underfoot. The ceiling was low; I had to duck slightly to avoid hitting my head.

The deeper I went, the less afraid I felt. It was strangely like that. If anything, the place felt nostalgic.

That's right, I thought. What I had feared wasn't dark places themselves. What I had feared, as a child, was being alone in the dark.

Before ten minutes had passed, I reached a dead end. At the base of the far wall, a half-rotted wooden box lay half-buried in the earth. I crouched down and brushed away the dirt. The lid opened surprisingly easily.

Inside was an old notebook with a black cover.

I opened the first page.

reiji

That was what it said.

In that moment, something rang clear and bright deep inside my chest.

---

Reiji.

A neighborhood kid, three or four years older than me. He always had scraped knees and a face that said he knew where all the secret places were. His pockets were always full of things—a butterfly net, magnets, marbles—and half of what he said was true and half was tall tales. I had loved trailing after him.

I hadn't forgotten him. I just hadn't been able to find the entrance back to that memory.

The notebook held records in a child's handwriting.

—Two entrances
—This side is shallow, the other side is long
—One bat
—No treasure yet
—Don't use matches

Along the edges of the pages were symbols that could have been maps or codes. I for entrance, H for the wide area, R for a narrow passage. Reiji had always loved writing things in initials, as if everything were a secret. I remembered how he used to fill the floor plans of our hideouts with cryptic letters. He'd wear that expression that said he only needed to understand it himself.

I turned the pages.

Partway through, the handwriting grew slightly more careful.

—It connects all the way through
—Need rope next time
—Show N
—He'll probably freak out
—But I'll show him anyway

When I saw those lines, I sank down to a crouch right where I stood.

N.

Naoto.

It was the initial of my name.

Even so, it took a little more time before the memory fully returned. Memory is a strange thing—sometimes knowing a name isn't enough to bring back the scene. As I stared at that single letter in the cold air of the cave, what surfaced first was a voice, rising from somewhere deep and distant.

Nao.

No, that wasn't it.

N.

I felt like there had been a more childish way he called me. But when Reiji wrote his secret maps and plans, he must have reduced even my name to an initial. He thought it was cool. That was the kind of kid he was.

---

I turned to the last pages of the notebook.

—Today we go all the way to the back
—N is coming
—There's a ledge over there so be careful
—He's like Spelunker so yeah

And there, at last, everything connected.

I remembered.

A summer afternoon. The entrance to the thicket. Reiji swinging his flashlight with a proud look on his face. Me in shorts and sandals, completely wrong for an expedition. Reiji looking at me and laughing—*you came dressed like that?* I made it a few steps inside and froze. It wasn't the dark hole itself that scared me. It was the first ledge, that drop down into the dark.

Reiji had said it.

"You're like the guy in Spelunker."

I hadn't known what it meant. But I could tell I was being teased, and it stung.

"I can do it."

I think that's what I said back.

Reiji laughed. He laughed, and then he said, *All right, next time I'll show you for real.*

That "next time" had been waiting inside the notebook.

I must not have made it to the back that day. That's why everything after that point had been cleanly excised from my memory. You can't remember a sight you never saw. But the fact that someone had wanted to show it to me—that had stayed, somewhere deep.

---

I closed the notebook and shone my flashlight on the dead-end wall.

Just an earthen wall. No gems, no secret door. Only dampness and time. But for a child, that was probably enough. The mere fact that it led underground made it another world.

At the bottom of the box, a few small things remained. A blue marble, a rusted bottle cap, a fragment of a plastic badge. Each too humble to call a treasure. And yet, Reiji had probably been dead serious about hiding something here.

I picked up just one marble. When I held it to the light, a thin bubble was trapped inside the old glass.

---

Outside, the summer light was blinding.

I reached for my phone and couldn't remember which pocket I'd put it in. I laughed a little, then checked the right front pocket. There it was. Sometimes I can't trust myself about something that happened seconds ago, yet I can vividly recall the smell of a thicket from decades past. I'm never sure which version of me is real.

But probably both are.

We don't remember everything. What matters most doesn't necessarily survive the longest. We don't store memories because they're useful. It's the places someone almost took us, the sequels we never got to see, the words we flung back without knowing what they meant—those are the things that linger, inexplicably.

Back home, I laid the dictionary and the notebook side by side on my desk.

In the dictionary, *spelunker* had a circle around it and *Spelunker!* scrawled beside it. In the notebook's final pages: *He's like Spelunker so yeah.* The same pencil strokes, bold and hurried. The same hint of pride.

It was Reiji who had circled that word.

He must have looked it up, found the meaning, and thought, *That's me.* He was thrilled that the word described someone who explores caves—just like him—and he couldn't resist writing it down. The Reiji of those days, who couldn't see a dark hole without wanting to climb inside, would absolutely have done that.

I closed the dictionary.

I'll probably never see Reiji again. I don't know when he moved away, or where he ended up, or what he's doing now. But a single word and three penciled lines on a scrap of paper were enough to bring back a childhood afternoon that had been out of reach for so long.

Underground, the world is only as large as the reach of your flashlight.

Memory might be a little like that, I thought. You can't illuminate everything at once. But from wherever the light falls, the shape of the path slowly becomes visible. And at the end of it, there's usually less treasure than you expected. Just a blue marble, a rusted bottle cap, and one word you never got to say.

But that's enough.

As a child, I was there. And Reiji was trying to show me what lay deeper inside. Just knowing that was enough to feel as though something that had been stalled at the entrance for a very long time had finally taken one step forward.

Queen Sacrifice

That day, I pulled off the first queen sacrifice of my life.

On the other side of the board, my opponent's hand stopped. His right hand, which had just pressed the clock, hovered in midair as if it had been about to reach for a piece and then forgotten how to move. I tried not to let it show on my face, but I could hear my own heartbeat far too clearly.

The tournament was being held on the second floor of a local community center, in a small meeting room. It was a simple setup: folding tables lined up in rows, chessboards placed on top of them, nothing more. Spring sunlight came in through the windows, casting neat shadows of the pieces across the boards. Along the wall, a few players who had already finished their games sat in pipe chairs and watched in silence. I felt that if I became aware of their eyes too much, I would fall apart, so I looked only at the board.

I was White, and I had just thrown my queen into my opponent's position.

Half a year earlier, I would never even have considered sacrificing my queen. The queen is the strongest piece on the board. It can move vertically, horizontally, diagonally. Lose it, and you are usually in trouble. When I had first learned chess as a kid, I treated the queen like something sacred. If it was in danger, I moved it away. If I could trade queens, I was happy to do it. I thought strong players were the kind who never lost their strongest piece.

But that wasn't how it felt anymore.

My opponent was a high school student named Takagi. He was one of the regular top finishers in tournaments like this, and he had even represented the prefecture the year before. When I saw his name on the pairing sheet before the round, I had honestly thought of this as a learning game. Even if I lost, I wanted to take something useful back with me.

And yet, once we entered the middlegame, the board began to look strange in a way I couldn't ignore.

Black's king had castled short, but there was a slight weakness in the pawn structure. The g-file was half-open. A diagonal for my bishop was already pointing toward the king side. My knight had jumped into the attack, and one of my rooks looked ready to join. In a position like that, when I would normally add pieces carefully and safely, an impossible move came into my head.

Put the queen on h7.

A move that let it be taken.

At first it looked like a joke, even to me. There was no way a move like that could really work—or so I thought. But the more I calculated, the more it seemed to hold. If he took the queen, his king would be dragged out of place. Then a knight check would come, then the rook would swing over, and eventually the mating net would close. At several moments it looked as though there might be an escape square, but each one turned out not to be real. At least, that was how it looked to me.

The problem was whether "it looked that way to me" was something I could trust.

If I had miscalculated, I would simply be down a queen and losing. Against someone like Takagi, that would mean getting cleanly cut down afterward. In the middle of a tournament, against a strong player, blundering everything away on a flashy move I had wanted to believe in—that was humiliating just to imagine. The version of me from before would never have done it.

At that moment, I remembered a conversation from the previous Thursday night.

I had been sitting in a café near the station with one of the older members from our club, replaying games on a board between our cups. He was a university student now, more on the management side of the club than the playing side, but sometimes he still took the time to work through positions with younger players like me. When I admitted that I lost confidence whenever the game turned sharp and attacking, he stirred a spoonful of milk into his coffee and said,

"People who only play safe moves usually lose safely."

"Lose safely?" I asked.

"Yeah. They spend all their energy trying not to collapse, and little by little the position gets worse, and by the time they notice, they're already lost. Of course reckless moves are bad. But if you can see a winning shape, then even if it scares you, you have to step into it."

Then he showed me a famous old game, one of those brilliant classics with a spectacular sacrifice in the middle. After the queen vanished from the board, the remaining pieces suddenly moved like living things, coordinating with each other as they drove the enemy king into a corner. Watching that game, I realized that a sacrifice was not something you played for beauty alone. You sacrificed because it was necessary. You gave up the biggest thing in the present because you trusted the shape of the future.

"But if you miscalculate, it's over," I had said.

He smiled.

"Yes. That's exactly why it's interesting."

I remembered those words in front of the board.

I took a deep breath and picked up my queen. The piece felt heavier than usual in my fingertips. The moment I placed it on h7, it felt as if the sound of the whole room had dropped by one level.

Takagi raised an eyebrow when he saw the move. After thinking for a little while, he took the queen, just as I had expected.

From there on, I was almost in a trance. Knight check. The king moved. I swung the rook over. Another check. The bishop's diagonal cut through the position. There was only one square left, and even that would be sealed by the next move.

There was one instant when the blood drained from my face. The real position looked slightly different from one of the branches I had been calculating in my head. But when I forced myself to count again calmly, the attack was still there. I had enough pieces. His defense could not catch up.

When I played the final move, Takagi stared at the board for a long time.

Then he let out a small breath and said,

"It's mate."

I think I nodded, but I'm not sure my body was really obeying me.

We shook hands, stopped the clocks, and filled in the result sheet. Each motion felt detached from reality. I had won. And not just won—I had won with a queen sacrifice. Even writing it down on the form made it feel like someone else's game.

When I stood up, an older man who had been watching from the wall said, "That was beautiful." I felt my face grow hot at once. Takagi, while packing up his things, gave a small, frustrated smile and said, "So you really saw all of that." There was nothing bitter in the way he said it. If anything, hearing it from a strong player made me straighten up a little.

At lunch break, I bought a sports drink from the vending machine and sat down on a bench in the hallway. Through the window I could see the yard of a nearby elementary school, where children who looked like first or second graders were running after a ball. I replayed the game again in my head. The moment I placed the queen. The moment he took it. The way the checks began to connect. It had definitely been frightening. But maybe because it was frightening, I had kept thinking all the way through, without stopping.

After a while, a message came from my senior.

—How'd it go?

I hesitated for a moment, then replied with as much calm as I could fake.

—I landed the first queen sacrifice of my life.

The answer came back immediately.

—That's one you'll remember forever.

I thought so too.

Someday I'll face stronger opponents. There will be positions far more complicated than this one. Things will not always go as perfectly as they did today. More likely, there will be many more days when I miscalculate and lose badly. Even so, I knew this game would remain.

For a long time, I had thought mostly about not losing things. Protecting important pieces. Keeping the position from breaking. Staying away from dangerous roads. Of course those things matter. But somewhere in every real fight, there is a view you can only reach by letting something go.

The queen is the strongest piece on the board. But the truly important strength was not the queen itself. It was whether I could trust the shape that would remain after I gave it up.

The call for the afternoon round began, and I stood up. I clipped the result sheet onto my board and started walking toward the next table. In the glass along the corridor, I caught sight of my own reflection. My face looked a little better than it had in the morning. I hadn't suddenly become strong. It was only that I was slightly different from the person I had been before daring to play a frightening move.

That day, I pulled off the first queen sacrifice of my life.

And maybe, more than sacrificing my queen, it was the first move in which I let go—just a little—of the version of myself that had always been afraid to lose.

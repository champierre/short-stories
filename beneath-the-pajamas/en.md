That day, I was going to sleep over at a friend’s house from elementary school, and for the first time I learned that French people sleep without wearing anything under their pajamas.

It felt less like a shock and more like the world had suddenly grown just a little wider.

After school, I dropped off my randoseru at home, and my mother took me to the little supermarket by the station to buy a toothbrush. It was the travel kind, in a clear plastic case. The friend I was staying with was Henri, a boy in my class. He was French, but his family had come to Japan when he was still very small, and his Japanese sounded just as natural as ours. He had only transferred to our school that spring, and maybe because of his name, everyone had made assumptions at first—that he must be good at soccer, or that he probably ate bread all the time. In reality, Henri ate natto with school lunch without any trouble, was not especially good at dodgeball, and was strangely talented at making odd little animals out of paper during art class.

Henri’s house stood in a quiet residential neighborhood a short walk from the station. It had white walls and blue shutters, with small olive pots lined up beside the front door. Henri’s mother opened it, smiled, said, “Bonjour,” and then added in slightly hesitant Japanese, “Hello. Come in.” I froze for a second before answering, “Hello.”

In the living room there was a large bookshelf, and mixed among Japanese and French books were thick volumes full of photographs of food and travel. On the table were French bread, Camembert cheese, and a tomato stew. Henri’s father, who seemed to use Japanese at work, spoke slowly but naturally and said, “Eat as much as you like.” His mother asked, after thinking for a moment, “Does it suit your mouth?” and Henri laughed right beside her and translated, “She means, is it good?” Henri said, “This is normal at our house,” but to me, none of it felt normal. Just tearing off a piece of bread, dipping it lightly into the stew, and putting Camembert on top made me feel as though I had come to some faraway country.

After dinner we played games in Henri’s room. Even though it was a Japanese game, every now and then, whenever an enemy appeared, Henri shouted something in French. When I asked what it meant, he said, in his usual easy way, “Stuff like ‘damn’ or ‘whoa’ or something like that.” I tried copying him, but I couldn’t say it right, and we both laughed.

A little after ten, Henri’s mother called out from downstairs. It was in French, so I couldn’t understand the words, but I could tell it meant bedtime because Henri stood up and answered, “Okay!” It seemed that inside the house, his mother always spoke to him in French. Right after that, Henri turned to me and said in smooth Japanese, “She says it’s time to sleep,” and for a moment I listened with a strange feeling to the way two languages flowed through a single home.

We brushed our teeth and stood side by side in front of the sink. In the mirror, I could see the two of us holding our pajamas. I started changing into the blue pajamas I had brought from home, and as usual, I kept my underwear on and was about to put on only the top first.

Then, with a completely ordinary expression, Henri took off his T-shirt, then his pants and underwear, and slipped straight into his pajamas.

It was so natural that for a second I couldn’t even understand what had happened.

“Huh?”

A weird little sound escaped my mouth.

Fastening his buttons, Henri looked at me as if to say, “What?”

“Under… there.”

“Under where?”

“Under your pajamas.”

Henri blinked, then finally understood what I meant.

“Oh,” he said.

“I don’t wear anything.”

“What?”

“Well, when you sleep.”

Suddenly I became aware of the elastic on my own underwear. Henri said it—*I don’t wear anything*—as casually as if he were talking about tomorrow’s weather. There was no embarrassment in it, and no pride. It was simply a habit, that was all.

“Is that how it is in France?”

“In our house, yeah. It’s always been that way since I was little. My mom and dad do too.”

“Since before you came to Japan?”

“Yeah. But I’ve lived in Japan since I was really small, so Japanese things feel normal to me too. Sometimes I don’t even know myself which parts of me are French and which parts aren’t.”

Then Henri thought for a moment and added, “But it’s not weird that you sleep with your underwear on.”

Even though I had been looking at him as if he were the strange one, he didn’t get angry. He said that first, and somehow that felt mysterious.

“Well, if it’s not weird, then okay,” I said.

But suddenly it felt as if there must be reasons behind the way my family slept and the way Henri’s family slept. Until then, I had never even considered that something as simple as what you wore to bed might differ from one household to another. I had assumed everyone slept more or less the same way my family did.

When we got into bed, I could hear the wind outside the window. Beside me, Henri said, “We’ll have chocolate bread for breakfast tomorrow.”

“Really?” I said, and after a short silence I asked, “Do people in France eat chocolate bread in the morning?”

“Sure. And in Japan people eat fish in the morning, right?”

That was true. I laughed.

As I looked up at the dark ceiling, I thought about how, at school, everyone sat at the same desks, moved at the same bells, and worked on the same kanji drills. But once we went home, dinner, bath time, bedtime books—everything was probably a little different. Even so, everyone thought their own way was normal. And when you stayed over at a friend’s house, that “normal” suddenly showed its face.

“Hey,” Henri said.

“What?”

“In Japanese anime, they drink milk after a bath, right? Is that true?”

“Not everybody.”

“I thought so. My mom saw that and thought all Japanese people did.”

From downstairs came the sound of dishes being put away. Henri’s mother said something in French, and his father answered with Japanese mixed in. The way their voices blended together somehow felt a little like Henri himself.

After a while, Henri’s breathing grew quiet. Maybe he had already fallen asleep. Under the blanket, I touched the waistband of my underwear. Maybe I really wouldn’t need it. But I didn’t have the courage to take it off. Knowing about a difference in culture and trying it yourself were not the same thing.

The next morning, white light slipped through the gap in the curtains. On the table there really was a crispy chocolate pastry, and every time flakes fell from the surface, I hurried to catch them on my plate. Henri’s mother drank coffee, and warm milk was set out for us.

“Did you sleep well?” she asked, looking slightly anxious.

“Yes, I slept very well,” I answered.

At that, she smiled with relief. Folding up his newspaper, Henri’s father said, “His Japanese is practically like a Japanese kid’s now, isn’t it?” Henri looked embarrassed and said, “Please stop saying it like that.”

On the way home, my mother asked, “So, how was it?”

I told her first that the games had been fun and that the bread had been good. Then, after hesitating a little, I said, “At Henri’s house, they don’t wear anything under their pajamas when they sleep.”

My mother said, “Really?” but she didn’t seem especially surprised.

“I guess different countries do things differently.”

The way she said it felt a little disappointing and a little reassuring at the same time. To me, it had felt like a major discovery, but to adults it was apparently just one of many differences that existed in the world.

Still, for me, it was enough.

For a while after that, every time I put on my pajamas at night, I remembered Henri’s completely ordinary face. And I thought about how the normal I knew was not the normal of the whole world, with a feeling that was a little proud and a little uneasy.

# Between Strategy and Ice

There is a particular frustration in curling: you can build the perfect strategy in your head, and still not be able to throw the stone the way you mean to.

Every time she stood on the ice, Aoi felt that frustration. In her mind, she could draw any number of ideal lines, each one stopping neatly at the back of the house. She could picture a path that slipped past the guard, kept her team’s stone alive, and even set up the next shot after that. If she wrote it down in a notebook, it would probably look like a solid plan.

But the moment she actually took the stone in hand, pushed off from the hack, and began to slide, that “probably” shattered all too easily.

A release just a little too early.
A turn just a little too strong.
A weight just a little too heavy.

On the ice, those tiny differences were fatal.

“Nice line! It’s still going!”

Maho, the skip, shouted. Shiori, Aoi’s sweeping partner, worked desperately with her brush. But as if betraying all their effort, the stone stopped half a meter away from where it was meant to be. It wasn’t a complete miss. It wasn’t enough to ruin the game. But it wasn’t right either. That in-between failure was the hardest kind to bear.

“Sorry.”

At the end of each end, Aoi said it under her breath.

Maho never got angry. She always just said, “Next one.” Shiori smiled and said, “That was better than the last.” Aoi knew they were being kind. But kindness sometimes dug deeper into her than the mistake itself. It made her feel as though she alone could see the full shape of her own inadequacy.

It was the winter of her second year in high school. The curling club was only in its fourth year and still could not be called a powerhouse. Even so, this year they had made it through the district qualifiers and, for the first time, reached the final four of the prefectural tournament. One more win and they would advance to a higher stage. They had come this far because of Maho’s calm judgment, Shiori’s powerful sweeping, and the steady effort of the first-year students.

Sometimes Aoi no longer knew what exactly she was contributing to any of it.

In strategy discussions, she talked more than anyone else. She noticed opponents’ habits quickly. During time-outs, if she suggested, “Instead of drawing one in here, I think we should make a thin take and leave the next shot easier,” Maho would often nod and say, “Let’s do that.”

And yet, the moment it became her turn to throw that very shot, her body seemed to recede from her. The strategy she had imagined stopped connecting to her hands and feet.

Their semifinal opponent at the prefectural championship was the defending champion.
After four ends, Aoi’s team was down by two. It was still a manageable gap, but the air around them was growing heavier by the minute.

Fifth end. They had hammer.

The opponents had placed an awkward center guard. One of Aoi’s team’s stones remained in front of the house in a delicate position. It was Aoi’s first shot of the end. Maho called for a draw that would pass beside the guard and stop near the button. If it got through, the position would not be bad. It might even become favorable.

“Usual weight. It’s narrow, but the line is there,” Maho said.
“I see it,” Aoi replied.

And she really did.
The question was whether she could actually throw it.

She crouched, gripped the handle, and took one breath. The lights of the rink reflected white across the ice, and the distant house looked faintly blurred. She had made this motion hundreds of times in practice, and yet the more important the moment, the more her body felt borrowed.

Push.
Slide.
Release.

The instant the stone left her fingertips, she knew. Slightly too heavy.

“Sweep!”
Maho’s voice rang out.

Shiori ran. The other sweeper chased after her. Their brushes scraped sharply across the ice. The stone made it past the guard—but once through, it did not stop in time. It drifted too deep, clipped the opponent’s well-placed stone, and scattered the arrangement in a messy half-measure. It was not a total disaster. But it was exactly the kind of breakdown they could least afford.

A small murmur rose from the opposing bench.
Something inside Aoi went cold.

During the time-out, Maho stared at the tactics board and said, “We’re still okay. We can fix it next shot.”
“…Sorry.”
“You don’t need to apologize.”
Maho looked up.
“I think right now, you’re throwing not to miss.”

Aoi had no answer.

“When ‘don’t fail’ comes before ‘put it here,’ it usually ends up just a little off,” Shiori said between breaths.
“But I get it. I do that too.”

It wasn’t comfort. Shiori was simply stating a fact. Somehow that made it harder for Aoi to bear.

The game stayed tangled until the late ends. Before the final end, they were down by one.
They could still win.
It could still reach them.

Final end. They had last stone.

After several exchanges, the house became crowded. Red and yellow stones overlapped, and guards were still in play. Before the last shot, they trailed by one. Two points would mean a come-from-behind victory, but it would not be easy. Before Maho’s final throw, Aoi’s second shot came around.

What she needed to do was slip through a narrow gap on the right, gently push the opponent’s shot stone, and leave her own team’s stone as second shot. It was not flashy, but it was the shot that would set up Maho’s last one.

“This one doesn’t need much weight, unlike before,” Maho said.
“Okay.”
“Don’t think about making it. Just deliver it.”

At those words, Aoi lifted her face a little.

Just deliver it.

Not to win.
Not to make up for the earlier miss.
Not to undo her mistake.
Not any of those big things.
Just deliver this one shot to the place it needed to go.

As she put her foot in the hack, Aoi realized for the first time that all day she had been looking too far ahead. Whether they would win. What would happen if she failed. Whether she would be blamed. Whether she would regret it. She had been staring at all of that, and not at the stone in her hand.

Push forward.
Slide with the breath.
Don’t lock the elbow.
Release.

The stone left her hand quietly.

“Yes, that’s good!”
This time, Maho’s voice did not feel far away.

Shiori and the others went into the sweep. The line was alive. The stone slipped through the narrow gap and touched the side of the opponent’s stone with a soft tap. A dry click echoed. The opponent’s shot stone shifted just a little, and Aoi’s team’s stone remained tucked inside it.

It wasn’t perfect.
But it was exactly the shape they needed.

At the edge of the rink, Aoi saw their adviser clench a fist ever so slightly.
She let out a breath where she stood. It felt as though one thread deep inside her chest had finally come loose.

After that, Maho made a beautiful last shot, and the team stole two points for the comeback win.
In the line of handshakes after the game, one of the opposing players said, “That last one was great.” Aoi could not manage much of a reply. “Thank you” was all she could say.

When they got back to the bench area, Maho tossed her a bottle.
“There. Nice shot.”
“That was only because your last one was amazing.”
“I couldn’t have made mine without yours first.”
Shiori nodded too.
“Your face changed at the end.”
“Huh?”
“It was like you were finally actually curling.”

Aoi thought they were teasing her, but both of them looked completely serious.

As she twisted open the bottle cap, she thought for a while.
She liked building strategy. She liked reading the house. She didn’t dislike thinking about the correct shape. But that alone wasn’t enough. Curling was a little like chess, and yet not chess at all. You could think of the right answer, and still have the hand that played it betray you on the ice. That was why it hurt. Perhaps that was also why it was so interesting.

Between strategy and skill, there was a far deeper gap than she had imagined.
But today, she felt she had learned—just a little—how to start building a bridge across it.

Don’t try to realize the whole ideal all at once.
Don’t throw just to avoid failing; throw to deliver the shot where it needs to go.
Don’t cling to the future result; stay in contact with the one throw in front of you now.

When she stepped out of the rink, it was already dark. The winter air was cold enough to hurt, and yet only her cheeks felt strangely warm. Whether they would make nationals or not, she still didn’t know. In the next game, she might once again fail to throw the way she wanted.

Even so, Aoi felt that she feared her own immaturity a little less than before.

There is a particular frustration in curling: you can build the perfect strategy in your head, and still not be able to throw the stone the way you mean to.

But perhaps the only place a true shot exists is beyond that frustration.

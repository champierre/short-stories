# The Lines of the Wind

“By watching the direction the birds are flying and the way the leaves on the trees are trembling, you learn to read the flow of the wind,” the paragliding instructor told me.

When I heard that, I felt a little let down. I had expected something more technical—meters, difficult theory, maybe a string of specialized terms. But the instructor standing before me, Takahashi, spoke about reading the sky as casually as if he were explaining how to take a walk.

“Of course we check the wind meter too. But the first thing we look at is what’s around us. You can’t see the air itself, right? So you watch the things it moves.”

The mountainside at the end of spring was covered in soft green. A narrow road ran below the training field, and beyond it stood several tall trees. Their leaves did not sway all at once. Each tree moved differently, just a little. The tree on the right suddenly rustled, and a moment later the tips of the branches on the middle one began to shake. Above them, two small birds glided in curved arcs.

Takahashi pointed.

“Over there. The wind is lifting them.”

To me, the birds only looked as though they were enjoying themselves. But when I looked again after he said that, I saw that he was right: they were hardly flapping at all. They were drawing circles without losing height, as if climbing an invisible slope.

“Can you see it?”
“...Sort of.”
“That’s enough for now.”

Takahashi smiled.

The reason I had signed up for the paragliding experience was not exactly that I wanted to fly. Or rather, it would be a lie to say I didn’t want to. But the real reason was more ordinary than that. It was the spring of my fourth year in college, and while everyone around me was making their plans—jobs, graduate school, the next clear step—I was the only one who couldn’t decide. No option felt decisive. Whatever I chose seemed likely to leave me with regrets, and part of me wanted to avoid choosing anything at all.

At a time like that, a friend jokingly told me, “You think too much. Go fly in the sky or something.” For some reason, those words sounded convincing, and a week later I was standing on a mountain training slope.

How to fasten the harness. How to tighten the helmet. What posture to keep when starting to run. Even as I listened to each explanation, half my mind was elsewhere. Should I stay in the lab or get a job at a company? Stay in Tokyo or go back home? Why did the field I had chosen for myself now feel strangely distant? Why were “what I like” and “what suits me” not necessarily the same thing?

Takahashi looked at my face and said suddenly,

“Nervous?”
“A little.”
“It’s fine. Everyone is.”
“It’s not just the flying. It’s... other things too.”

As soon as I said it, I thought I had revealed too much. But Takahashi did not look surprised. He only nodded as he looked past me toward the slope.

“We get a lot of people like that.”
“Really?”
“Not everyone comes just to fly. Some come because they want to decide something. Some come because they can’t.”

I laughed before I could stop myself.

“Does flying really help people decide?”
“Usually not,” he said.
“Exactly.”
“But sometimes it helps them understand what they’re afraid of.”

He picked up a thin blade of grass from the ground and released it from his fingertips. Instead of dropping immediately, it lifted lightly for a moment, then drifted slowly to the right.

“The wind doesn’t move in a straight line. It rises, falls, curls, disappears all at once. So if you try to read it perfectly, it gets difficult. What you read is only how it’s flowing here, right now.”
“...I see.”
“If you think too much about what comes next, your feet stop moving. Especially when you’re flying.”

That line went straight into me.

By afternoon, it was finally time for the tandem flight. I would be in front, Takahashi behind me. Below the slope the town spread out in miniature: square rice fields reflecting light, a silver river, tiny cars moving along the roads. The sky was high, with thin clouds floating here and there.

“All right. When I give the signal, run. Don’t try to sit down. Keep running until the ground disappears.”
“Okay.”
“Don’t stop halfway.”
“Okay.”
“The wind is fine. We can go.”

I had no idea whether it was really fine or not. But Takahashi’s voice alone sounded strangely calm.

“Ready. Look ahead. Run!”

One step, two, three. I ran down the slope. Behind me I felt the fabric filling with air. The dirt beneath my feet moved farther away. I tried to take one more step—and there was no ground there anymore.

I don’t even know if I shouted.

It wasn’t that my body fell. It was lifted. My stomach lurched coldly, and in the next instant the view opened all at once. The slope, the trees, the people—everything was below me. The wind streamed past my cheeks, humming low by my helmet.

“You still breathing?”
I heard Takahashi call from behind.
“I am!”
“Good. Then look at the view.”

Only then did I finally look down. The place where I had been standing a few minutes earlier was already far away. Seen from above, the trees’ movements looked like ripples spreading across the ground. The sunlight struck the leaves differently, making their colors vary just a little, and the brighter streaks almost seemed to mark the paths where the wind was passing.

In the distance, a bird crossed slightly above us. Without moving its wings much at all, it sailed smoothly along the slope.

“Is that bird reading the wind too?” I asked.
Takahashi laughed.
“Maybe it doesn’t read it so much as know it.”
“That seems unfair.”
“But humans have our own way of seeing.”

After a while, I realized I had forgotten to be afraid. Not that the fear was gone. It just no longer came first. Before that, my attention had shifted to where I was now and which way I was being carried.

To know the invisible through the visible.

Maybe that wasn’t only true of wind. The future itself cannot be seen. But the small ways my heart responds can be seen: what moves me, what makes me take one small step forward, what makes my feet stop. Those faint tremors are visible after all.

It wasn’t that I had come to hate research itself. But when I imagined a future of staying in one place for a long time, my breathing grew shallow. On the other hand, when I thought about explaining things to someone, or making things while trying different ideas, my body leaned forward just a little. For a long time I had dismissed that feeling as meaningless.

“Takahashi?”
“Yes?”
“The wind can change suddenly too, right?”
“It can.”
“What do you do then?”
“If you realize it’s changed, you adjust.”
“That’s all?”
“That’s all. Don’t expect it to stay the same.”

For some reason, that answer made me laugh. Maybe I had been making something simple much harder than it was.

The landing was quiet, almost anticlimactic. The ground came near, my shoes brushed the grass, and after two or three running steps, the time in the sky was over. When I took off the harness, I noticed my legs were trembling a little, but the feeling soon passed.

As I handed back the helmet beside the training field, Takahashi asked,

“So? How was it?”
“It was... much more than I expected.”
I searched for the words.
“I think I learned that it’s okay not to understand everything yet.”
“That’s good.”
“You said the only thing to read is how it’s flowing here and now, right?”
“That’s right.”
“I don’t think I’ll forget that for a while.”

While waiting for the bus back, I took out my phone. A few notifications had arrived. One was a message from my professor, saying I didn’t need to rush my answer about graduate school and that I should come talk whenever I wanted. Another was from someone at the company where I had interned before. Apparently they had a small project they wanted help with, separate from the formal hiring process.

Before, I think I would have tried to answer both of them immediately. I would have panicked, thinking I had to choose the correct one.

But that day, I put my phone away and looked up at the sky first.

The bird I had seen earlier was gone. Instead, the flag above the training ground was slowly changing direction. A moment later, the leaves in the trees stirred after it. Something invisible was passing through them, unmistakably.

I took a deep breath.

There was no need to decide everything today. All I needed was not to lose the sense of what lifted me, even slightly, in one direction or another.

Until the bus arrived, I checked the wind a few more times: the air against my cheek, the way the grass by the road bent, the faint hum of the power lines. Even after coming down from the mountain, the world was still flowing in the same way. And I felt I could believe that a little more than before.
